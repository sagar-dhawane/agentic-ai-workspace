Opening Constructive Arguments in Favor of:
“The cases of Unnao, Hathras, and RG Kar show that India has strong laws for women’s safety, but the real problem is a corrupt system of politicians and police protecting powerful criminals.”

Argument 1 — The legal framework for women’s safety is robust and substantive, designed to deter and punish sexual violence, and capable of delivering justice even when powerful actors are involved
- Core laws exist and were purpose-built to protect women and children. Key statutes include:
  - The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act, 2012, which creates strict penalties for sexual offenses against minors and obligates child-protection oriented procedures in cases involving young victims.
  - The Indian Penal Code (IPC) provisions that define and punish rape (including aggravated scenarios), along with strengthening amendments in 2013 to widen the scope of sexual offenses and enhance sentencing.
  - The Criminal Law (Amendment) Acts (notably 2013 and subsequent updates) that broaden definitions of sexual violence, increase penalties, and introduce harsher punishment for repeat or aggravated offenses.
  - Vishaka guidelines (precursor to workplace sexual harassment protections) and ongoing jurisprudence that establish concrete duties for institutions to prevent and address gender-based violence.
- Real-world implications of the statute architecture:
  - The Unnao case illustrates how national-level legal frameworks can be activated to pursue accountability when a case involves a powerful political actor. A Supreme Court–led or government-ordered move to bring the case under central investigation (CBI) demonstrates the law’s potential to override local impediments and pursue justice across power lines.
  - The Hathras case likewise triggered robust legal and public accountability mechanisms—demands for independent investigation, judicial scrutiny, and policy-level reflection on how survivors’ cases are handled by police and prosecutors. The ensuing discourse confirmed that the law provides the routes for inquiry, postmortem integrity, and liability for mishandling evidence.
- Up-to-date reality (in structure, not isolated incidents): Across states and at the national level, India has created legal instruments and enforcement pathways intended to safeguard women’s safety, including speedy-trial incentives, stronger evidence standards for sexual violence, and mandatory institutional response obligations for workplaces and authorities. The existence and deployment of these tools in high-profile cases show that the framework itself is capable of delivering substantive accountability when properly activated.

Argument 2 — Independent investigative and judicial institutions have repeatedly demonstrated the will and capacity to apply the law against powerful actors, proving that accountability is achievable even under political pressure
- Centralized, independent investigations exist as a corrective mechanism to political interference. The Unnao case is a prime example:
  - When local policing and political pressure risked shielding a powerful suspect, a decision to transfer the case to the CBI (under Supreme Court direction) showcased the system’s ability to step in, override local influence, and pursue a formal, impartial inquiry.
  - Subsequent judicial processes culminated in accountability for the accused, reinforcing the message that political protection is not a shield against the law.
- The Hathras case catalyzed national-level judicial and parliamentary attention and spurred calls for independent inquiry and transparent investigation, underscoring:
  - The judiciary’s readiness to demand due process, protect the rights of the victim and family, and ensure investigations are conducted in a fair and supervised manner.
  - The willingness of central and state machinery to respond to public scrutiny by invoking oversight mechanisms and revisiting investigative protocols when police handling is questioned.
- RG Kar (as part of the broader pattern) reflects that, in cases involving institutions or influential actors, accountability channels exist and can be activated through courts, commissions, or central agencies when there is political will and public pressure. The key takeaway is that the system has demonstrated, in multiple instances, its capacity to override attempts at obstruction and to move toward judicial, evidentiary, and prosecutorial accountability.
- Takeaway from the 2026 landscape: The existence of independent inquiry bodies, Supreme Court oversight, and central investigative agencies, and the demonstrated willingness to use them in high-profile sex-offense cases, confirm that the laws are operative and capable of producing accountability—even where political power is invoked to shield offenders.

Argument 3 — The persistent problem lies in enforcement and systemic corruption, not in the absence of strong laws; the remedy lies in reforms that sever the nexus between crime and political-police protection
- Core claim: The laws are strong; the weakness lies in how effectively they are enforced when powerful interests are at stake. The patterns observed in Unnao, Hathras, and similar high-profile cases point to a recurring problem: political backing, police inertia, and procedural bottlenecks can stall investigations, delay justice, or deflect accountability.
- Evidence of enforcement gaps in high-profile cases:
  - In several cases, local police handling drew widespread criticism for delays, procedural lapses, or perceived influence by powerful individuals. Critics argue that such missteps undermine public faith in the criminal-justice system and embolden perpetrators who count on political protection or bureaucratic inertia.
  - Civil society, media, and judicial forums have consistently pressed for reforms to safeguard survivor rights, ensure witness protection, and shield investigations from political interference. The demand for independent inquiries, timely postmortems, and transparent prosecution is a direct response to these enforcement gaps.
- Concrete reforms that flow from these arguments:
  - Police reforms: Professionalization, anti-corruption measures, and clear accountability for mishandling cases; insulated complaint mechanisms for survivors to ensure swift and unbiased action.
  - Witness protection and survivor support: Strengthened legal protections, safe reporting channels, and relocation options where necessary to protect those who come forward.
  - Fast-track, transparent trials: Dedicated, adequately staffed fast-track mechanisms for sex-crime cases with clear time-bound timelines and public reporting on progress to reduce pendency.
  - Data transparency and civil oversight: Public dashboards on case progression, convictions, and outcomes; independent bodies to monitor law-enforcement performance in gender-based violence cases.
- Bottom line: The strength of India’s laws is not in doubt, but the effectiveness of their application is contingent on removing political and police impunity for powerful offenders. The Unnao and Hathras cases, along with the broader pattern they reveal, make a compelling case for targeted reforms that sever the incentive structures enabling corruption and shielding criminals. With robust reform, the existing legal architecture can translate into consistent, credible justice for survivors.

In sum, the three core points converge on a simple yet powerful thesis: India does have a formidable, comprehensive legal framework for women’s safety, and it has the mechanisms—courts, central agencies, and accountability processes—to enforce it. The repeated, publicized cases including Unnao and Hathras demonstrate that when these mechanisms are activated and allowed to operate free from political and police protection, substantive accountability follows. The remaining obstacle is the entrenched system of corruption and political protection that curtails enforcement—precisely the issue the RG Kar-era and similar cases illuminate. The path forward is clear: maintain and strengthen the strong laws, and expunge the corrupt practices that shield criminals, so that justice for survivors is not a rare exception but a routine outcome.
